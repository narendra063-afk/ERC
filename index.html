Part B · Mandate & institutional design

What the Commission is actually for.

Established under the Electricity Regulation Commission Act, 2074 and Regulation, 2075 — an autonomous body created to make Nepal's power sector organised, transparent and competitive.

Vision

Become a distinguished, trusted, capable regulator for Nepal's power sector — protecting consumers and producers alike while strengthening the energy sector.

Tariff regulation

Set fair, cost-reflective tariffs and service charges that protect consumers while letting producers and distributors recover legitimate cost — the Commission's single largest responsibility.

Market & open access

End the single-buyer monopoly: guarantee third-party open access on the national grid and grow a wholesale power market.

Stakeholder engagement

  • Public hearings — mandatory before any tariff change or major policy shift, opened to consumers, industry, civil society and energy experts.
  • Draft & comment — new directives and reports are published in draft first, so stakeholders can weigh in before adoption.
  • Dispute resolution — the Commission sits as a quasi-judicial bench (इजलास) for commercial and technical disputes among producers, distributors and consumers.

Institutional independence

  • Statutory authority — appointment and removal of commissioners follow fixed legal procedure and qualification criteria, insulating decisions from political pressure.
  • Financial & operational autonomy — the Commission runs its own regulatory functions, staffing and internal procedures independently, needed to stay neutral between consumer and producer interests.
Part C, §1.3 · Choosing a model

Why a hybrid model — segment by segment.

Pure free markets and pure state monopoly are both judged unsuitable for Nepal's seasonal hydropower swings, geography and economy. Toggle between today's reality and the proposed hybrid model to see how each segment should move along the spectrum.

Reading the hybrid model: generation opens up to private and foreign capital under transparent licensing and PPAs, while transmission & distribution — too capital-intensive to duplicate — stay centrally regulated, but with mandatory third-party open access. An independent commission and cross-border trading alignment hold the whole system together.

Command & control

Direct government control of price and entry. Predictable, but slow to attract capital or efficiency.

Self-regulation

Industry sets its own rules. Lightweight, but weak on consumer protection without a strong enforcer.

Incentive-based — chosen for Nepal

Part ङ · नवाचार र उदीयमान मुद्दा

What's arriving next on the grid.

Four technologies the plan flags as reshaping the sector, and three emerging risks regulation hasn't fully caught up with yet.

Innovation

Smart grid & metering

NEA is replacing conventional meters nationwide — enabling real-time consumption data, faster leak detection and remote connect/disconnect.

Innovation

Battery & pumped storage

Run-of-river hydropower can't meet winter peaks alone — BESS and pumped storage bank wet-season surplus for dry-season demand.

Innovation

EV charging & smart load

A fast-growing EV fleet is fed by a national network of smart charging stations that discounts off-peak hours to balance grid load.

Innovation

Green hydrogen

Early-stage pilots use spilled monsoon-season electricity to produce hydrogen — a future path to fertiliser and industrial fuel self-sufficiency.

Emerging risk

Cyber security & data privacy

Smart meters, online billing and SCADA systems widen the attack surface — the plan calls for a national cybersecurity protocol for the grid and the Load Despatch Centre.

Emerging risk

Climate & disaster exposure

Floods, landslides and glacial lake outburst floods (GLOF) threaten hydropower assets — climate-resilient design and mandatory insurance are proposed for new builds.

Emerging risk

Energy mix & dynamic tariffs

Over-reliance on hydro causes winter shortages — growing solar and wind into the mix needs a time-of-day / dynamic tariff framework to keep the grid balanced.

Part D, §6 · Challenges & mitigation

What could go wrong — and the plan's answer.

Institutional resistance

NEA may resist open access and wholesale market reform, fearing the loss of its single-buyer monopoly.

Mitigation: high-level policy dialogue via the Ministry, building confidence in long-term financial sustainability

Technical capacity gap

Neither the Commission nor NEA currently has the staff to run a wholesale market or sophisticated trading software.

Mitigation: donor-funded training (World Bank, ADB) with exposure to power exchanges in India and Europe

Political & policy instability

Frequent government change risks shifting energy policy priorities mid-implementation.

Mitigation: lock these reforms into a new Electricity Act so they survive leadership change
